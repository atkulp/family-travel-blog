Now that we have four weeks of school behind us, we can share a more holistic view of what we&#8217;re studying!

Our classmates are super diverse, not only in country of origin, but also educational background. Many have been devout Buddhists for years. Many new students have never written academic papers. Some have undergraduate degrees in Buddhist Studies and are here to get graduate degrees. Some are monks and nuns who memorize religious texts on a regular basis. Some older people have left careers in the West to deepen their religious practice. The vast majority speak English as a second language. I&#8217;m amazed that it works to cram us all in the same classroom and give us the same assignments.

## Khenpo Class

We have Khenpo class (a.k.a. Textual Studies 101, taught by a highly educated Tibetan monk) every day for 1.5 hours, plus a weekly 1-hour review class with the translator. (Side note: we enjoy hearing the translator speak Tibetan with Khenpo with her strong Russian accent.) We study one book ‚Äî Shantideva‚Äôs *Bodhisattvacharyavatara* ‚Äî line by line. When we leave after four months of study, we will only be halfway through the book! Along the way, we draw on Tibetan Buddhist commentaries to learn theories, terms, lists, and more.

‚ÄúBodhisattvacharyavatara‚Äù translates to ‚ÄúThe Way of the Bodhisattva‚Äù. A bodhisattva is a being who generates *bodhicitta*, the wish to attain enlightenment for the sake of liberating all sentient beings from the painful cycle of rebirth. The goal in the Mahayana tradition, the largest branch of Buddhism, is to become an enlightened bodhisattva. Shantideva wrote this foundational text in the eighth century to guide ordinary beings on the bodhisattva path. ## Nepali Language

RYI takes its language classes seriously! We hear that Colloquial Tibetan, Classical Tibetan, and Sanskrit classes are much more difficult than Nepali, but we&#8217;re still working hard. We have an hour of &#8220;Master Class&#8221; with our lovely professor three times a week, an hour of one-on-one practice with native speakers twice a week, and an hour of &#8220;Boost Class&#8221; with four students and two native speakers once a week. That&#8217;s a total of six hours of language class every week. Since the class sizes are so small, we&#8217;re constantly put on the spot to speak individually. With all the classes, homework, daily life with a Nepali host family, and interactions with Nepali people around town, we&#8217;re learning pretty quickly.
