Homestay: Here to Stay

On Saturday, we OSU students packed up our things, checked out of Shechen Guest House, trekked in the heat to RYI (we really don’t recommend walking with rolling suitcases in Kathmandu), met our host families, and headed home with them for the first time!

Gabriel and I are staying in a three-story house in a fancy part of town with two brothers (Roshan and Milan) who work in the financial sector, their mother (Ranju), Milan’s 10-year-old son (Ishan), and at least one servant (Saili). Roshan is married, but his wife and son live in another house with his in-laws (and now Mesa from OSU!). The third floor consists of Roshan’s bedroom, our bedroom, a shared living room, a shared bathroom and a nifty two-level balcony. Roshan and Milan speak English pretty well, Grandma and Ishan speak a little, and Saili speaks none, but I’ve bonded a lot with her just by smiling and gesturing.

The family members belong to the highest Hindu caste — Brahmin — so Gabriel and I, as outcastes, have to avoid ritually polluting them or their home with our presence. We stay out of the kitchen and shrine area. Menstruating women aren’t allowed in the kitchen. (Nepal is mostly Hindu, so it’s not a surprise that we’re staying with a Hindu family. We’re studying Buddhism, though, which does not have a caste system.)

Milan and Roshan have crazy busy work schedules, so we’ve spent by far the most time with Grandma. Saili and Grandma serve us food and clear our dishes. It’s a constant struggle to insist that we’re full and don’t need any more food! Grandma sits with us as we eat. She used to teach Nepali language, so she has taken it upon herself to teach us a bunch! Between Nepali lessons during RYI orientation and informal lessons with Grandma, I estimate we’ve learned 100 words so far. Not bad for three days.

The food is DELICIOUS. They cook nearly the same thing for lunch and dinner every single day: dal (lentil soup), bhat (rice), tarkari (vegetable curry), Nepali salad (just cucumber and carrot), and achar (various kinds of spicy pickles, analogous to salsa). It’s my favorite cuisine, so I don’t mind the repetition at all. Plus, you can imagine that you get really good at cooking something when you make it twice a day for decades! I really hope that Grandma or Saili will agree to give us cooking lessons sometime.

Side note: our intrepid guide Dr. Barstow went home to Oregon a week ago. It was great having him along, but we don’t mind the increased independence now that we have our feet under us!
