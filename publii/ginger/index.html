Ginger

Some people may know that Japan, and Asian countries in general, like their ginger. It’s used frequently in many different dishes and is popular in candy and drinks. Ginger also plays a particularly large role in sushi, where the reddish picked slices are eaten with pretty much any form of rice and fish.

Since ginger is so popular, and since I rather enjoy eating ginger, we thought it might be fun to try to make some of our own pickled ginger. The process isn’t that hard and makes some pretty yummy ginger!

The first step is peeling the ginger root. The skin is tough and fibery. Also, we don’t have a potato peeler or anything, so I had to use our knife to cut the skin off. Overall, this was the most time consuming and difficult step (it’s really not a hard process).

The next step is to cut the ginger into thin slices. There’s nothing precise about this step, we just want small pieces to make it easier to eat with stuff later. Our mandolin doesn’t have any settings for thickness, so our slices are thicker than you’d usually find at a restaurant.

Next we need to put the slices in a jar and prepare our pickling juice. Now I should mention that we’re not really pickling the ginger in the normal way. We’re going to do what’s called a quick pickle. The brine/juice is a mix of vinegar, water, sugar, salt, and spices such as anise if that’s your jam. We boiled the mixture with ginger sprouts, but they didn’t really give much flavor.

Once the mixture has been brought to a boil and left to simmer for a few min, the juice/brine is poured over the ginger and allowed to cook for a few min before sealing it and putting it in the fridge. Since this isn’t a real pickle, we don’t have to worry about gas build up in the jar – the container can be sealed and the pickle will brine for a few days at minimum.

For those that don’t know, raw ginger is quite strong and fibery. The vinegar and sugar will help sweeten and subdue the strong ginger flavor as well as break down the fibers a bit. That said, it’s still very gingery and has lots of fibers, just not as bad as raw.

In the end, the ginger didn’t turn quite as red as I was expecting, but apparently that’s due to the age of the ginger, younger = more pink. Though 2 days is the minimum for good ginger this way, ours sat for 2 weeks and is much better for it! The flavor is very good, still stronger than most restaurant grade stuff, but I prefer strong ginger.

Random ginger facts from the internet, pickled ginger has slight anti-microbial properties which may help in the consumption of raw food (fish mostly). Also, the vibrant pink often seen in restaurant ginger is because they add food coloring!
